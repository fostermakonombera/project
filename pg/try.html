INTRODUCTION
The ABO blood grouping system was discovered by Karl Landsteiner in 1901. This
system has proved to be a fundamental classification system for human blood. The
system categorises blood based on the antigens found on the red blood cells. The
ABO system has proved to be crucial for blood transfusion, organ transplants, and
understanding paternity.
HOW ARE THE BLOOD GROUPS ARE INTERPRETED?
Blood groups are interpreted based on the presence or absence of specific antigens on
the surface of red blood cells. These antigens can trigger an immune response if they
are foreign to the body. The ABO system uses this property to categorise blood into
four major groups: A, B, AB, and O, determined by the antigens A and B. The system
entails that if someone has blood group A then they have Antigen-A on their cells. If
one has blood group B, they have antigen B. And if they have blood group AB, they
have both antigens A and B. Presence of neither antigen means they have blood
group O. Additionally, the Rh factor, which can be positive or negative, further
divides these groups based on the presence or absence of the Rhesus antigen
(American Red Cross).
THE BASIS OF INHERITANCE OF ABO BLOOD GROUP.
The ABO blood group system is based on the presence or absence of two antigens,
A and B, on the surface of red blood cells. These antigens are determined by a single
gene with three different alleles: IA, IB, and i. The IA allele codes for the A antigen,
the IB allele for the B antigen, and the i allele does not produce any antigen.
The possible combinations of these alleles result in four blood types:
- Type A: Individuals have either two IA alleles (IAIA) or one IA and one i allele (IAi).
- Type B: Individuals have either two IB alleles (IBIB) or one IB and one i allele (IBi).
- Type AB: Individuals have one IA and one IB allele (IAIB), expressing both A and B
antigens.
- Type O: Individuals have two i alleles (ii), expressing no A or B antigens.
The IA and IB alleles are codominant, meaning that both alleles express their antigens
when present together. The i allele is recessive to both IA and IB alleles.
UNIVERSAL BLOOD GROUP
O negative blood is considered universal because it can be safely transfused to
individuals of any ABO blood type. The reason for this universal compatibility lies in
the absence of A, B, and Rh antigens on the red blood cells of O negative individuals.
Without these antigens, the recipient’s immune system has no foreign antigens to react
against, reducing the risk of an adverse transfusion reaction. This makes O negative
blood especially valuable in emergencies and for patients with rare blood types,
where matching blood may not be readily available.
MISMATCH IN BLOOD TRANSFUSION
If an individual receives a blood transfusion with the wrong blood type, their immune
system will recognize the donor red blood cells as foreign. This recognition is due to
the presence of antigens on the surface of red blood cells that are not matched
between the donor and recipient. The recipient’s immune system will produce
antibodies against these foreign antigens, leading to an immune response. The
antibodies will bind to the antigens on the donor red blood cells, causing
agglutination (clumping) and hemolysis (destruction) of the red blood cells. This
immune reaction can result in a transfusion reaction, which is a serious and potentially
life-threatening event. Symptoms can include fever, chills, back pain, dark urine, and a
rapid decrease in blood pressure. The severity of the reaction depends on the volume
of incompatible blood transfused and the speed of the immune response.

It provides a solid platform for all blood related research and advancements.
